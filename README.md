The problem of diseases with high mortality rates remains a critical issue for Turkey's healthcare system, where delayed diagnosis and inefficient hospital resource allocation are directly correlated with mortality. The goal of our project is to develop an early warning and risk assessment system for the most common fatal diseases — diabetes, hypertension, and stroke — using non-laboratory indicators, and to optimize the management of medical resources based on WHO statistics and existing research. Unlike existing approaches that address disease prediction and hospital resource allocation as separate problems, or rely on costly laboratory data inaccessible outside clinical settings, the proposed framework integrates both into a single pipeline that operates exclusively on self-reportable indicators, making early screening accessible to broader populations without the need for clinical infrastructure. The system is based on a two-stage machine learning pipeline. For the first layer a multi-class XGBoost model, trained on a synthetic dataset with 30,000 records generated based on expert medical knowledge consolidated from actively practicing clinicians and large language model consultations, determines the patient's dominant pathology. Following this, disease-specific models calculate personalized risks: for diabetes, XGBoost combined with CalibratedClassifierCV provides a 0–100% risk score; for stroke, an ANN optimized to minimize false negatives ensures high recall; and for hypertension, Logistic Regression with calibrated probability thresholds is applied. In these models, "Green" indicates the absence of significant deviations, "Yellow" indicates negative behavioral patterns requiring correction, and "Red" signals a critical probability of disease requiring immediate intervention. The selection of threshold values is mathematically justified by the need to balance precision and recall for each condition. In the next stage, we developed a resource management optimization algorithm that routes patients by calculating a multifactorial hospital rating, taking into account transport distance and real-time workload of the medical facility. The algorithm dynamically adapts to external factors such as weather conditions, peak traffic hours, and national holidays in Turkey. The results of modeling and resource allocation are integrated into a unified web application. To validate the models using real-world data, a structured questionnaire was developed in accordance with ethical standards and approved by the Ethics Committee of Ankara Yıldırım Beyazıt University, with sample sizes statistically determined using G*Power at a significance level of 0.05 and power of 0.95, resulting in target figures of 1,258 respondents for hypertension, 662 for diabetes, and 2,043 for stroke. In conclusion, our study presents a framework that combines disease classification, risk prediction, and resource allocation simulation, with the goal of increasing patient survival rates through preventive monitoring and optimization of emergency medical services.
